01 · The problem & the economics

Why this wins

The 30 days after hospital discharge are the highest-risk medication window in healthcare. Med lists change at discharge — new drugs added, old ones stopped, doses altered — and patients leave confused. Medication problems in this window are a leading driver of 30-day readmissions.

Here's the part that makes this a business and not a wellness app: this phone call already exists, is already mandated, and is already paid for. Medicare reimburses clinics for Transitional Care Management (TCM), which requires contacting the patient within two business days of discharge — and Medicare penalizes hospitals for excess readmissions. Today those calls are made manually by nurses, and many clinics leave the reimbursement on the table because they can't staff them.

Our one-line pitch: "Clinics are paid to make these calls and can't staff them. Hospitals are penalized for the readmissions these calls prevent. We automate the call — and escalate the judgment to humans."

This maps directly onto the hackathon brief: a full conversation end-to-end, grounded in domain knowledge (the patient's actual discharge med list), personalized to each caller, with reliability and guardrails as the bar. And it maps onto the host: Arya Health's thesis is the economics of healthcare services. This is a reimbursed-workflow automation story, not a chatbot story.

03 · The conversation

The call, step by step

Six beats. Every yes/no question also accepts keypad input, so a noisy demo room can't kill us.

1 · GREET

Identity + transparency. "Hi, is this Margaret? This is the care assistant calling on behalf of Dr. Chen's office after your hospital stay." Clearly an assistant — never impersonates a human.

2 · PICKUP

Prescription pickup check. "You were prescribed a new medication, Eliquis. Were you able to pick it up from the pharmacy?" No → AMBER: coordinator gets an SMS. This one question catches one of the most common post-discharge failures.

3 · NEW MEDS

Adherence walk-through, grounded in the discharge list: new meds started, changed doses confirmed.

4 · STOPPED

The stopped-med trap — our jaw-drop moment. "The hospital stopped your old warfarin. Just to confirm, you're no longer taking that one?" Still taking both = RED. This is a real, dangerous, common error, and catching it live is the demo highlight.

5 · SYMPTOMS

Red-flag screen tied to the meds: 2–3 yes/no questions ("any unusual bruising or bleeding?"). Yes → RED.

6 · CLOSE

Warm sign-off + log. Outcome written to the status board. Also honest framing for judges: a "yes" confirms self-report, not ingestion — and self-report with escalation still beats the status quo by miles.

04 · System design

Architecture

Telephony

Twilio Programmable Voice + SMS

Outbound calls via the Voice API; ConversationRelay / Media Streams for streaming voice if we go free-form. SMS for all escalations. (Twilio = sponsor prize requirement.)

Brain

Open model, constrained (Gemma / MedGemma)

A small open model classifies patient replies (yes / no / unclear / clinical question) — via Ollama locally, Groq, or Google AI Studio, swappable by one env var, with a regex fallback that keeps calls alive if every API dies. It does NOT choose the flow — the state machine does. This is our reliability story, verbatim.

Server

FastAPI or Express

Holds patient records, the state machine, call/session state, escalation logic, and the dashboard endpoint. One process, one file if we want.

Fallback

DTMF everywhere

Every question answerable by keypad. Free-form voice is a layer on top, and we decide at rehearsal which mode goes on stage.

Tunnel

ngrok

Twilio webhooks → our laptop. Set up early; it's the classic time sink.

Data

Two hardcoded patients

Margaret (clean, all green) and Harold (still taking his stopped warfarin → red). Two patients = the personalization demo.

07 · Scoring strategy

Rubric map — hit every scored line

Criterion (1–5)Our answer
Technical implementationState-machine-governed LLM ("the LLM phrases, the state machine decides"), DTMF fallback on every question, idempotent escalation, graceful degradation. Production-shaped by design.
Idea uniquenessNot a reminder app — a discharge-window safety net that catches the stopped-med error class, built on a workflow that's already mandated and reimbursed (TCM) but unstaffable.
Team explanationThe economics story: Medicare pays for the call, penalizes the readmission; nurses are the bottleneck. One sentence, everyone on the team can say it.
UI/UXVoice designed for elderly users: slow pace, one question at a time, keypad fallback, transparent "I'm an assistant" framing. Plus the nurse dashboard: triage at a glance.

Anticipated judge pushback — have answers ready. "Isn't this just an IVR with an LLM?" → The value is the grounding (patient-specific med list), the error-class detection (stopped meds, duplicates), and the closed loop to a human. "How do you know he really took it?" → We don't — self-report plus escalation, and we say so. "HIPAA?" → Named as a production requirement: BAA-covered infra, consent at discharge, no PHI in logs. "Long-term vision?" → This is the wedge of a full personal medical secretary — we built the episode where it saves lives and gets reimbursed first.

08 · Sponsor stack

How we use Twilio, Lovable & Anthropic

"How you used Twilio" is a required line on the Devpost submission, and Twilio must carry the telephony to qualify for sponsor prizes. We use it four ways — say that number in the pitch.

Twilio · 1

Programmable Voice (outbound)

Server hits the Voice REST API to place the call. Twilio requests TwiML from our webhook (via ngrok) to drive the conversation.

Twilio · 2

<Gather> — structured mode

Captures DTMF keypresses and basic speech on every question ("press 1 for yes"). Bulletproof in a noisy demo room. Build this first, always works.

Twilio · 3

ConversationRelay — free-form mode

Call connects to a WebSocket on our server; Twilio handles STT/TTS streaming, we receive/send text. Exactly the product the AI Startup Searchlight celebrates — sponsor-depth points. Layer on top if time allows.

Twilio · 4

Programmable Messaging (SMS)

Every amber/red escalation is a Twilio SMS to the nurse/coordinator phone — the judge's-hand moment. Twilio carries both halves of the loop: conversation and escalation.

Lovable

Nurse dashboard + landing page

Prompt Lovable to generate the React triage board (patient cards, green/amber/red, escalation feed) pointed at our JSON endpoint — polished UI in ~15 min, buys UI/UX rubric points. Optional 5-min landing page for the Devpost link. Lovable renders state only; it never touches core logic.

Open model

Gemma / MedGemma — the constrained brain

Interprets messy patient speech ("pharmacy didn't have it, daughter's picking it up Thursday" → pickup pending, AMBER). Health-tuned open model = great pitch line + self-hostable for PHI in production. Provider ladder: regex fallback → Ollama local → Groq (fastest hosted) → Google AI Studio. The state machine owns all flow and escalation decisions.

Trial-account trap: Twilio trial accounts can only call verified numbers. Verify every team + demo phone the moment we sit down. Decide at rehearsal whether stage mode is Gather (safe) or ConversationRelay (impressive) — Gather ships first regardless.
